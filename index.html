📦 What is a Packet?

Your message is broken into small pieces called packets — like tearing a letter into 10 pieces. Each piece travels a different route and they're reassembled at the destination.

🏠 What is an IP Address?

Every device on the internet has an IP address — like a home address. Without it, the internet wouldn't know where to deliver your data.

📒 What is DNS?

"google.com" is easy to remember. But computers work with numbers. DNS converts "google.com" into 142.250.80.46 — its actual address.

🌊 Underwater Cables?

Thousands of kilometers of fiber optic cables run under the ocean floor — connecting India to Singapore, USA, Europe. That's how global data travels in milliseconds.

Every time you see technology — ask these 5. Always.

01
What does it DO?

Human + business purpose. What problem were they solving?

Day 1 · Block 5 · 30 mins

Inside a Computer

Not as hardware specs — as a story everyone can understand.

⏱ 30 minutes
Ask the Class 👇

"When you open a game on your laptop — what's actually happening inside the box?"

🧠 CPU — The Brain

Processes every single instruction. When you click "open game" — CPU figures out what to do next, millions of times per second.

School analogy → The Principal who makes every decision

📋 RAM — The Desk

What's open RIGHT NOW. When you have 20 Chrome tabs open, they all sit in RAM. Close the laptop → RAM is cleared. Like clearing a desk.

School analogy → The classroom where work is happening NOW

💾 Storage — The Almirah

Files stay here forever — even when turned off. SSD = fast, HDD = slow but big.

School analogy → The library — everything stored permanently

🎨 GPU — The Artist

Handles all visuals. Every pixel when playing BGMI — the GPU is drawing all of it, 60 times per second.

School analogy → The art teacher who handles all drawings

🛣️ Motherboard — The Roads

Connects everything. CPU, RAM, GPU, Storage — they all talk to each other through the motherboard. No motherboard = nothing connects = computer is useless.

School analogy → The corridors that connect all departments

Discussion Question
"When your computer becomes very slow — which part do you think is the problem and why?"
RAM is full (too many tabs) → slow. SSD is slow → slow to open files. CPU is old → slow at processing. Let students guess and debate.

Day 2 · Case Study 1 · 35 mins

🍕 Zomato — The Hungry Idea

Started by scanning paper menus. Now delivers to millions daily.

⏱ 35 minutes

Deepinder Goyal, working at a consulting firm in 2008. The office cafeteria had paper menus. People wasted 10-15 minutes just deciding where to eat. He saw this every single day.

📅 How Zomato Was Born

2008
Deepinder scans paper menus and puts them on a website called "Foodiebay". No delivery — just menus online. Colleagues loved it immediately.
2010
Renamed to Zomato. Expanded from Delhi to Mumbai, Bengaluru, then more cities. Still just restaurant discovery — no delivery.
2015
The big pivot — added food delivery. Built a fleet of delivery partners. This changed Zomato from a directory to a platform.
2021
IPO — listed on stock market. Valued at ₹60,000+ crore. A company that started with scanned menus.

What happens when you place a Zomato order? Click ▶ to walk through:

1
You place the order
App sends request to Zomato's server with your location, restaurant, and items
2
Algorithm finds nearest delivery partner
Real-time GPS scan — assigns closest available partner within 500m
3
Restaurant gets notification
Restaurant's tablet beeps — they start preparing. Prep time is tracked.
4
ETA is calculated live
Traffic data + prep time + distance = "Delivery in 28 mins" (updates every 30s)
5
Delivery partner picks up & rides
GPS tracked live. Off-route → system flags automatically
6
Order delivered — payment settled
Zomato takes 18-25% commission from restaurant. Partner gets paid.
Ask the Class
"Why does Zomato charge more during rain or festivals?"
Demand goes up, supply goes down. Algorithm increases price to attract more delivery partners online. This is called Surge Pricing.

The founder was stranded on a highway. That became a billion-dollar company.

⏱ 25 minutes
The Origin Story 👇

Bhavish Aggarwal booked a cab in 2010. The driver stopped midway, demanded extra money. Bhavish refused. The driver left him stranded on the highway.

He didn't just get angry and move on. He asked: "Why is this system so broken? Can I fix it?"

💡 The Insight

"What if cab booking was standardized — fixed price before you book, GPS tracked the whole way, driver is accountable with ratings?"

🛺
Added Auto-Rickshaws
Cabs were for the rich. Autos made Ola accessible to everyone — including Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities like ours.
💵
Cash Payments
Unlike Uber which was card-first, Ola allowed cash. In India, most people didn't have cards in 2011. This was a massive advantage.
🎁
Driver Incentives
Ola paid drivers bonuses to join. More drivers → shorter wait times → more users → more orders → platform grows.

The CEO Mindset

3 principles every founder thinks about. Every day.

⏱ 10 minutes
01
Problem first. Product second.
Don't build an app and then find users. Find a real pain — then build the solution. Zomato found the pain (menu confusion). Ola found the pain (unreliable cabs).

Day 3 · How Apps Really Work · Full Session

An App Is a System,
Not Just a Screen.

Behind every app, there is a system: user input, server, database, logic, response, and update.

⏱ Full Day Session
Ask the Class 👇

"When you open Instagram and like a post, what really happens? Do you think the heart just becomes red and that's all?"

No.
Your tap becomes a signal.
That signal goes to Instagram's server.
The server checks who you are.
It updates the like count in the database.
Then it sends the updated count back to your phone.
Then your screen shows the new number.
So one small tap from your side causes many hidden steps.
That means apps are not random. Apps follow a process.

Every App Has 5 Parts

01
📥 Input
What does the user do? Click, type, upload, search, scroll, pay.
02
⚙️ Processing
What decision should the system take?
03
💾 Storage
Where is the information saved?
04
📤 Output
What result is shown to the user?
05
🔄 Feedback Loop
Did it work? Should it update something else?

🎥 YouTube Example — All 5 Parts

You search "cricket highlights" — that is Input. YouTube checks your request — that is Processing. It finds matching videos from stored data — that is Storage. It shows you results — that is Output. Then it remembers what you watched — that is the Feedback loop.

"Stop seeing apps as entertainment only. Start seeing apps as systems. The students who understand systems today will understand coding much more easily tomorrow. Because coding is nothing but telling a system: If this happens, do this. Then save this. Then show this."

Class Activity — Break an App into 5 Parts
Ask students to pick one app they use daily and answer all 5 questions for it.
Pick 3 students. Let them explain with WhatsApp, YouTube, or Google Maps. Input → Processing → Storage → Output → Feedback. Do not correct — let them think it through.
✅ Day 3 Key Takeaways
An app is a system, not just a screen
Every app has input, processing, storage, output, and feedback
Hidden logic is what makes digital products powerful
Understanding systems is the first step toward coding
📌 Homework / Observation Task
Open any 3 apps at home and write: what action you performed and what hidden steps might have happened behind it
→ How This Connects to Day 4
Today students saw that apps follow systems. Tomorrow they will learn that systems work well only when instructions are clear and in sequence. That leads naturally into algorithm thinking.

Day 4 · Step-by-Step Thinking · Full Session

Step-by-Step Thinking:
The Power of Sequence

Technology works because instructions are given in the correct order. Machines cannot guess.

⏱ Full Day Session
Start With This 👇

"If I say, 'Make tea,' can a machine do it? Not unless I give exact steps."

Boil water. Add tea powder. Add milk. Add sugar. Wait. Filter. Pour.
Machines do not understand "make tea." Machines understand steps.
Humans can guess. Machines cannot.
If your mother says, "Bring that thing from there," you may still understand. But a computer will not. A computer needs exact instruction.

"In life, sequence matters. In studies, sequence matters. In business, sequence matters. In technology especially, sequence matters a lot."

Day 5 · How Systems Make Decisions · Full Session

How Systems Make Decisions

If this happens, do this. Otherwise, do something else. This is the foundation of all logic.

⏱ Full Day Session
Core Idea 👇

"Life is not always one straight road. Sometimes a system must decide. If password is correct, open account. If password is wrong, show error."

🚦 Traffic Signal Logic

If timer becomes zero → switch light. If ambulance detected → maybe override signal. If road is empty → in smart systems, timing may reduce. Even machines make decisions based on rules.

📺 YouTube Recommendations

If you watch cricket videos often → recommends more sports. If you stop watching a channel → slowly reduces those suggestions. Systems check conditions constantly.

🔐 Phone Face Unlock

If face matches → unlock. If face does not match → stay locked. Simple. Powerful. Logical.

🏫 School Attendance Software

If student is present → mark P. If absent → mark A. If fee not paid → show pending. If marks are below cutoff → send alert.

"You should begin asking: What is the rule here? What happens if the rule is true? What happens if it is false? This thinking is very powerful. Because most digital products are built on conditions."

Activity — If This, Then That
Give students these situations and ask them to answer both possibilities:
• If it rains, what happens in food delivery pricing?
• If payment fails, what should the app show?
• If internet is slow, what should YouTube do?
Ask them to write: If [this], then [that]. Otherwise [what].
✅ Day 5 Key Takeaways
Systems follow rules
Decision-making is based on conditions
"If this, then that" is a major technology principle
Logic begins with simple conditions
📌 Homework
Write 5 daily-life examples of: If [something] happens → what is the result? Example: If battery is low → phone shows warning.
→ How This Connects to Day 6
Today students learned about conditions. Tomorrow they will see how companies and apps use data to make decisions instead of guessing.

Using the Internet Smartly,
Not Blindly

Scrolling is not research. Forwarded messages are not facts. Using internet is not equal to learning from internet.

⏱ Full Day Session
Important Distinction 👇

"Most students use the internet every day. But very few use it smartly. Using internet is not equal to learning from internet."

✅ Internet CAN Help You

Learn skills, watch explanations, compare products, search information, find opportunities, discover careers, build knowledge.

❌ Without Direction, It Wastes Time

Scrolling random content. Sharing unverified forwards. Watching videos without purpose. Screen time that builds nothing.

Before opening the internet, ask:
Why am I opening this?
What do I want to find?
How will I know this is correct?
This is digital maturity.
Suppose you search: "Best way to improve memory." You will find many videos. But are all correct? No.
Some people want views. Some want to sell products. Some give half knowledge.
Smart students check: Who is saying this? Is it reliable? Do multiple sources say the same thing?

🛡️ Digital Safety — Non-Negotiable Rules

✗Do not share OTP with anyone
✗Do not click random links in messages
✗Do not trust every screenshot or forwarded message
✗Do not post everything publicly
✗Do not use the same weak password everywhere

"Technology gives power. But without awareness, power becomes risk. From today, I want you to become responsible users. Not scared. Not addicted. Aware."

Activity — Useful vs Wasteful vs Risky

Searching maths solution → Useful. Watching 2 hours of random reels → Wasteful. Clicking unknown prize links → Risky. Learning Canva from YouTube → Useful. Sharing OTP to "bank staff" → Dangerous. Let students debate and explain their reasoning.

Internet can educate or distract — the choice is yours
Smart search needs purpose and verification

Day 10 · AI Awareness · Full Session

AI Is a Tool,
Not Magic

Some people think AI is magic. Some think it will replace everyone. Some use it blindly. All three are dangerous.

⏱ Full Day Session
Three Wrong Ideas 👇

"Some people think AI is magic. Some think AI will replace everyone. Some use it blindly. All three are dangerous. Let us understand it properly."

AI means systems that can perform tasks that look intelligent.
Answering questions. Recognizing faces. Suggesting videos. Translating language. Predicting what you may want next.
But AI is still a tool.
If the user gives poor input → output may be poor.
If the data is wrong → AI can mislead.
If you use it lazily → your thinking becomes weak.
Use AI as an assistant, not as a brain replacement.

😴 Weak Student Uses AI Like This

"Give answer."
That's all. No thinking involved. Result: The student learns nothing and becomes dependent.

🧠 Smart Student Uses AI Like This

"Explain in simple language." "Give me an example." "Compare both options." "Turn this into steps." "Check mistakes in my work." "Help me understand, not just answer."

"AI becomes more useful when your questions become better. The future will not only belong to those who know AI. It will belong to those who know how to think with AI."

Activity — Good Prompt vs Bad Prompt
Give one topic like "solar system" or "Zomato." Ask students: what is a weak question? What is a smart question?

Day 11 · Problem Solving · Full Session

Problem Solving
Like a Builder

Big problems become easier when broken into parts. This is the real skill under all technology.

⏱ Full Day Session
The Core Skill 👇

"Until now we learned systems, sequence, conditions, data, structure, communication, internet, AI. But what is the real skill under all this? Problem solving."

Technology is built because problems exist. So a strong learner must ask:
What is the real problem here?
What are its parts?
Which part should I solve first?

✏️ Live Example — Students Forget Homework Often

Why does it happen?
• No reminder system• No planner or habit• Too many subjects• No parent follow-up
Possible solutions
• Daily checklist on board• Homework reminder app• Class WhatsApp group reminder• Simple progress board

When the problem was one big block → it felt difficult. When broken into parts → it became workable.

"No big product is built in one shot. It is broken into smaller tasks. If you say 'I want to build an app,' that is too big. Instead ask: What problem? Who is the user? What is the first feature? What should happen first? That is builder thinking."

Activity — Break the Problem
Ask groups to choose one local problem and break it down completely.
Options: water wastage in school, late school bus, homework confusion, messy classroom, long canteen queue. Each group answers: What is the problem? Why does it happen? What small solution can help right now?

Big problems become easier when broken into parts
Builder mindset means asking better questions
Technology starts with problem clarity
Small solutions can solve large issues over time

⚠️ Where Workflow Breaks

Registration happened but confirmation not sent. Payment done but name not added. Order placed but restaurant did not receive. Strong companies reduce workflow breaks. Strong students also reduce workflow breaks in their own work.

"Without knowing it, you are already walking toward coding mindset. Input comes. System checks. Process happens. Output goes. Done. That is workflow. That is also code."

Activity — Draw a Flow
Ask students to draw arrows for one workflow using boxes on paper or a whiteboard.
Options: ordering food → joining an online class → withdrawing cash from ATM → sending an email. Each box = one step. Arrows connect the steps. Where could it break? Mark those in red.

Now tell me: What is coding?
Coding is simply writing instructions for a computer in a language it can understand. That's all.
You already know the thinking. Now we are slowly moving toward the language.
Suppose I say: "If student is present, mark attendance. If not, show absent."
That is logic. In coding, the same idea is written in a formal way.
Suppose I say: "Take two numbers and add them."
That is also instruction.
So Python is not a monster. It is just a clean, beginner-friendly language that helps us convert our thinking into working steps.

🐍 Why Python?

✓Reads almost like English — easy to learn
✓Used for AI, websites, games, automation
✓Beginner-friendly — short, clean code
✓Free and open source — anyone can start
Activity — Human Logic to Computer Logic
Say normal statements and ask students to convert them into step logic. No Python syntax yet — only thinking.
• "Ask name, then greet" → Step 1: Take name. Step 2: Print "Hello" + name.
• "Ask marks, then say pass/fail" → Step 1: Take marks. Step 2: If marks ≥ 35, print Pass. Else print Fail.
• "Ask age, then say child/teen/adult" → Step 1: Take age. Step 2: If age < 12, child. If 12–18, teen. If 18+, adult.

"People who jump into coding without thinking get confused quickly. You are not doing that. You are building foundation first. That is why your learning will be stronger. From next stage onward, when we start Python basics, I want you to feel: 'I already understand systems. I already understand steps. I already understand conditions. Now I am just learning how to write them in Python.' That is the correct mindset."

✅ Day 13 Key Takeaways
Python is a natural next step, not a sudden jump
Coding is simply writing logic in a language a computer understands
The real foundation of coding is thinking — not syntax
Students are now mentally ready to begin Python basics

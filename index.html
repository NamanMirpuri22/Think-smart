📦 What is a Packet?

Your message is broken into small pieces called packets — like tearing a letter into 10 pieces. Each piece travels a different route and they're reassembled at the destination.

🏠 What is an IP Address?

Every device on the internet has an IP address — like a home address. Without it, the internet wouldn't know where to deliver your data.

📒 What is DNS?

"google.com" is easy to remember. But computers work with numbers. DNS is the phone book that converts "google.com" into 142.250.80.46 — its actual address.

🌊 Underwater Cables?

Yes! Thousands of kilometers of fiber optic cables run under the ocean floor — connecting India to Singapore, USA, Europe. That's how global data travels in milliseconds.

Day 1 · Block 2 · 25 mins

The 5 Questions Framework

Every time you see technology — ask these 5. Always.

⏱ 25 minutes
01
What does it DO?
The visible function. What happens when you use it?
02
HOW does it do it?
The mechanism. What's the system underneath?
03
What POWERS it?
Energy or data source. What fuels this system?
04
What if ONE part FAILS?
Dependency thinking. What breaks if X stops?
05
Who built this and WHY?
Human + business purpose. What problem were they solving?
🎯 Live Practice — Apply to the Fan/AC in this Room
Q1 What does it do? → Moves air to cool the room
Q2 How? → Electric motor spins blades, motor converts electricity to rotation
Q3 Power? → Electricity from grid → transformer → your room's wire
Q4 Failure? → Capacitor fails → motor won't start. Wire cut → no power.
Q5 Why built? → Humans in hot climates needed cooling without ice. Invented 1882.

Day 1 · Block 3 · 30 mins

Inside a Computer

Not as hardware specs — as a story everyone can understand.

⏱ 30 minutes
Ask the Class 👇

"When you open a game on your laptop — what's actually happening inside the box?"

🧠 CPU — The Brain

Processes every single instruction. When you click "open game" — CPU figures out what to do next, millions of times per second.

School analogy → The Principal who makes every decision

📋 RAM — The Desk

What's open RIGHT NOW. When you have 20 Chrome tabs open, they all sit in RAM. Close the laptop → RAM is cleared. Like clearing a desk.

School analogy → The classroom where work is happening NOW

💾 Storage — The Almirah

Files stay here forever — even when turned off. Your photos, videos, games are all stored here. SSD = fast, HDD = slow but big.

School analogy → The library — everything stored permanently

🎨 GPU — The Artist

Handles all visuals. Every pixel on your screen when playing BGMI — the GPU is drawing all of it, 60 times per second.

School analogy → The art teacher who handles all drawings

🛣️ Motherboard — The Roads

Connects everything. CPU, RAM, GPU, Storage — they all talk to each other through the motherboard. No motherboard = nothing connects = computer is useless.

School analogy → The corridors that connect all departments

Discussion Question
"When your computer becomes very slow — which part do you think is the problem and why?"
Hint: RAM is full (too many tabs) → slow. SSD is slow (old laptop) → slow to open files. CPU is old → slow at processing. Let students guess and debate.

What Is a Company, Really?

Before Zomato and Ola — understand what a company actually is.

⏱ 15 minutes
Ask the Class 👇

"Raise your hand if your family has ever ordered food online or booked a cab online. Now — do you know the story of HOW those companies started?"

😣
A Problem
Something that frustrates many people every day
💡
An Insight
"What if there was a better way?"
🏗️
A Solution
Product + team + money + technology

"Every CEO's first job is not to make money — it is to find a real problem and obsess over solving it. The money comes later, if the solution is good enough."

👥 People (Team)

Engineers, designers, salespeople, operations. A great idea with no team stays an idea forever.

💰 Money (Funding)

Investors give money in exchange for a share of the company. Called "equity." Zomato raised billions before it was profitable.

⚙️ Technology

The app, website, backend servers, algorithms — the tools that make the solution work at scale.

📊 Business Model

How does money come IN? Without this, it's a charity, not a company. Every company must answer this.

Day 2 · Case Study 1 · 35 mins

🍕 Zomato — The Hungry Idea

Started by scanning paper menus. Now delivers to millions daily.

⏱ 35 minutes

Deepinder Goyal, working at a consulting firm in 2008. The office cafeteria had paper menus. People wasted 10-15 minutes just deciding where to eat. He saw this every single day.

📅 How Zomato Was Born

2008
Deepinder scans paper menus and puts them on a website called "Foodiebay". No delivery — just menus online. Colleagues loved it immediately.
2010
Renamed to Zomato. Expanded from Delhi to Mumbai, Bengaluru, then more cities. Still just restaurant discovery — no delivery.
2015
The big pivot — added food delivery. Built a fleet of delivery partners. This changed Zomato from a directory to a platform.
2021
IPO — listed on stock market. Valued at ₹60,000+ crore. A company that started with scanned menus.

What happens when you place a Zomato order? Click ▶ to walk through it:

1
You place the order
App sends request to Zomato's server with your location, restaurant, and items
2
Algorithm finds nearest delivery partner
Real-time GPS scan — assigns closest available partner within 500m of restaurant
3
Restaurant gets notification
Restaurant's tablet beeps — they start preparing. Prep time is tracked.
4
ETA is calculated live
Traffic data + prep time + distance = "Delivery in 28 mins" (updates every 30 seconds)
5
Delivery partner picks up & rides
GPS tracked live. If partner goes off route — system flags it automatically
6
Order delivered — payment settled
Zomato takes 18-25% commission from restaurant. You get a receipt. Partner gets paid.
Ask the Class
"Why does Zomato charge more during rain or festivals?"
Answer: Demand goes up (everyone wants delivery), supply goes down (fewer delivery partners willing to ride in rain). Algorithm increases price to attract more partners online. This is called Surge Pricing.

Day 2 · Case Study 2 · 25 mins

🚕 Ola — Born from a Bad Cab Ride

The founder was stranded on a highway. That became a billion-dollar company.

⏱ 25 minutes
The Origin Story 👇

Bhavish Aggarwal booked a cab in 2010. The driver stopped midway, demanded extra money. Bhavish refused. The driver left him stranded on the highway.

He didn't just get angry and move on. He asked: "Why is this system so broken? Can I fix it?"

💡 The Insight

"What if cab booking was standardized — fixed price before you book, GPS tracked the whole way, driver is accountable with ratings?"

🔧 What Made Ola Grow Fast

🛺
Added Auto-Rickshaws
Cabs were for the rich. Autos made Ola accessible to everyone — including Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities like ours.
💵
Cash Payments
Unlike Uber which was card-first, Ola allowed cash. In India, most people didn't have cards in 2011. This was a massive advantage.
🎁
Driver Incentives
Ola paid drivers bonuses to join the platform. More drivers → shorter wait times → more users → more orders → platform grows.
Ask the Class
"Why does Ola's price go up on New Year's Eve at midnight?"
Demand is extremely high, supply (available drivers) is low — many drivers have gone home. Algorithm increases price to attract more drivers to come online. This is the same surge pricing concept as Zomato. Now connect it: all platforms use this same logic.

Day 2 · Case Study 3 · 20 mins

🌍 Uber vs Ola

Same idea. Different markets. Why local knowledge wins.

⏱ 20 minutes

Travis Kalanick was in Paris, 2008. Snowy night. Couldn't find a cab for an hour. He thought: "What if you could press a button and a car appears?" That became Uber.

Feature UBER OLA
Founded2009, San Francisco2010, Mumbai
Original marketUSA — premium usersIndia — all classes
PaymentCard only at firstCash + card from day 1
Vehicle typesCars only initiallyCars + Autos + Bikes
Indian city knowledgeHad to learn & adaptBuilt for India natively
Driver relationsStruggled in IndiaDeep driver network

🏆 Key Lesson — Localization Wins

Uber came to India with billions of dollars. Ola beat them in many cities — not because Ola had more money, but because Ola understood the Indian customer better. Autos, cash, Hindi-speaking support, festivals pricing — all things Uber ignored initially.

📊 What both companies actually sell

Neither Ola nor Uber owns a single car. They are platforms — they connect drivers who have cars with passengers who need rides. They make money on every trip (15-25% commission). Their product is trust and convenience — not transportation.

Think About This
"Zomato doesn't own a single restaurant. Ola doesn't own a single car. How are they worth thousands of crores?"
They own something more valuable — the platform, the users, the data, and the trust. The network of restaurants/drivers is their real asset, even if they don't own them. This is the "platform economy."

Day 2 · Closing · 10 mins

The CEO Mindset

3 principles every founder thinks about. Every day.

⏱ 10 minutes
01
Problem first. Product second.
Don't build an app and then find users. Find a real pain that real people have — then build the solution. Zomato found the pain (menu confusion). Ola found the pain (unreliable cabs).
02
Obsess over the user.
Uber lost to Ola because Uber didn't understand the Indian user. Ola understood that Indians pay cash, ride autos, speak local languages, celebrate festivals. Know your user better than they know themselves.
03
Fail fast. Fix fast.
Zomato started with just menus in one office. Ola started in just Mumbai. Small start, fast learning, quick fixing. You don't need a perfect plan — you need to start, observe, and improve.
Final Question for the Class 👇

"What problem in your school, home, or neighbourhood do you see every single day — that nobody has solved yet?"

Give them 2 minutes to think silently. Then ask 3-4 students to share. Don't evaluate. Appreciate every answer. Some will have genuinely brilliant observations.
